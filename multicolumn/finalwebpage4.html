Do The Right Thing Poster and Synopsis

Salvatore “Sal” Fragione is the Italian owner of a pizzeria in Brooklyn. A neighborhood local, Buggin’ Out, becomes upset when he sees that the pizzeria’s Wall of Fame exhibits only Italian actors. Buggin’ Out believes a pizzeria in a black neighborhood should showcase black actors, but Sal disagrees. The wall becomes a symbol of racism and hate to Buggin’ Out and to other people in the neighborhood, and tensions rise.

Famous Quote:

One hand is always fighting the other hand, and the left hand is kicking much ass. I mean, it looks like the right hand, Love, is finished. But hold on, stop the presses, the right hand is coming back.

Movie Review

Do the Right Thing (1989) is Spike Lee’s intense, thought-provoking exploration of racial tension in a Brooklyn neighborhood on one hot summer day. The film dives deep into the lives of its characters, each representing different sides of the racial divide, and shows how small frustrations can build up into something explosive. The heat in the air isn’t just physical—it’s an emotional pressure cooker. Lee captures that feeling perfectly with vibrant colors and a pace that keeps everything simmering until it boils over.

The performances are fantastic, especially Danny Aiello as Sal, the pizzeria owner who’s stuck in the middle of the conflict, and Lee himself as Mookie, whose actions end up playing a major role in the film’s explosive climax. You really feel the tension in every interaction, and that’s what makes the ending so impactful—it’s inevitable but still gut-wrenching.

Lee’s direction is spot on, using visuals and music to build tension and urgency. The heat of the day mirrors the growing racial and social frustrations, with Public Enemy’s “Fight the Power” fueling the anger. Lee’s storytelling isn’t just in the dialogue—his visuals drive the film toward its intense climax. Do the Right Thing stays with you, prompting reflection on race, personal choices, and the complex nature of doing what’s right in a world full of injustice.

Director

Spike Lee is a director who doesn’t hold back. His films tackle tough topics like race, identity, and social justice head-on, using his unique style to make a real impact. Lee’s direction is bold and his camera work often brings energy and emotion to every scene. He’s not afraid to make people uncomfortable, pushing the audience to think deeply about the world around them. Lee's films stand out because he blends powerful storytelling with strong visuals, making sure his message resonates long after the movie ends.
